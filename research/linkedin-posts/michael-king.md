## Post 1

**Published:**
1 month ago (approx. June 2026)

**Source:**
https://www.linkedin.com/posts/michaelkingphilly_beyond-rag-why-every-ai-search-platform-activity-7462828552837820416-NHCA?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAF0RUjQB7CqMw72pOMtnLMPI-1pRJ3oqRoE

### Summary

Michael discusses how major AI platforms have moved beyond simple RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation). He explains that the "retrieve-once-then-generate" pattern is dead, replaced by agentic RAG pipelines with multiple gatekeepers where content can easily get filtered out.

### Key Takeaways

- Traditional RAG architectures are obsolete for major platforms like ChatGPT Search, Perplexity, and Google Deep Research.
- Content now has to survive multiple "gatekeepers" (often black boxes) rather than a simple top-k retrieval process.
- SEOs need to understand agentic RAG pipelines to diagnose where their content is being dropped.

### Why It Matters

To effectively produce content for AI search engines, we must optimize for complex, multi-step agentic pipelines rather than traditional keyword or basic semantic retrieval. Understanding these gatekeepers is critical for maintaining visibility.

## Post 2

**Published:**
1 month ago (approx. June 2026)

**Source:**

### Summary

In this post, Michael critiques Google's official guidance on AI Search. He warns the SEO community against naively celebrating the guide as permission to continue "business as usual," arguing that doing so relegates SEOs to being the "janitors of the web."

### Key Takeaways

- Google's official AI search guidance is self-serving and shouldn't be blindly trusted.
- The assumption that optimizing for AI search is "just SEO" is naive and dangerous.
- SEOs must evolve their strategies rather than relying on outdated playbooks endorsed by search engines.

### Why It Matters

When producing AI-powered content, we cannot rely solely on Google's official guidelines. We need to actively test, innovate, and develop independent strategies to secure visibility, rather than passively maintaining traditional SEO practices.

## Post 3

**Published:**
1 month ago (approx. June 2026)

**Source:**
https://www.linkedin.com/posts/michaelkingphilly_quick-tip-the-case-for-cloaking-for-large-activity-7460696588773134336-IzBn?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAF0RUjQB7CqMw72pOMtnLMPI-1pRJ3oqRoE

### Summary

Michael argues for a reevaluation of "cloaking" in the age of AI crawlers. Because AI systems consume proprietary content without sending meaningful traffic back, he suggests technical SEOs must selectively make content accessible. He advocates for protecting moats (datasets, research) while ensuring citation-worthy content is still visible to AI.

### Key Takeaways

- AI crawlers break the traditional web bargain of exchanging content for traffic.
- It is necessary to protect proprietary data and internal research from being freely consumed by LLMs.
- Technical SEO is shifting towards selective accessibility (controlling what AI systems can see based on user agents and rendering).
- Markdown optimization is becoming relevant for fast AI consumption.

### Why It Matters

Our AI content production strategy must include defensive mechanisms. We need to structure our websites so that AI engines can easily ingest our marketing content (for citations in AI Overviews), while deploying technical barriers (edge computing, selective rendering) to protect our high-value, proprietary assets from being scraped without compensation.
