## 1. Stop Trying to Replace People with AI
- **Type:** AI Strategy / Positioning Article
- **Source:** Growth Memo (Substack)
- **Link:** https://www.growth-memo.com/p/stop-trying-to-replace-people-with
- **Summary:**
Kevin Indig addresses the positioning mistake of marketing AI as a replacement for human employees ("substitution positioning"). He points out that despite early predictions by AI tech executives (like Sam Altman and Dario Amodei) that software engineering and customer service jobs would vanish, demand in these fields has actually risen. Layoffs and replacement tactics damage company culture, generate employee resistance, and result in low-quality "workslop." Indig advocates for positioning AI as an "enhancement" to amplify productivity rather than cut headcount.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - **Substitution Positioning Decay:** Marketing AI as a replacement wins short-term attention but erodes trust with buyers and employees, causing brand damage.
    - **The Jagged Frontier:** Knowledge workers achieve 25.1% faster execution and 12.2% more output by understanding where AI excels and where it fails. Real value lies in directing the tool, not leaving it on autopilot.
    - **Productivity ROI vs. Headcount Reduction:** NBER research indicates that using AI to enhance human output yields a significantly higher ROI than firing specialized staff (who are needed to review and audit AI-generated content).
    - **Human Option Value:** Layoffs justified by AI automation often backfire (e.g. Klarna walking back customer support cuts due to lower quality and customer demand for human agents).

## 2. The Ghost Citation Problem
- **Type:** AI Search Industry Study
- **Source:** Search Engine Journal
- **Link:** https://www.searchenginejournal.com/the-ghost-citation-problem/572453/
- **Summary:**
A study analyzing 3,981 domains across 115 prompts and 4 engines (ChatGPT, Google AIO, Gemini, AI Mode) to explore "Ghost Citations." This refers to instances where an AI engine uses a website's content and provides a source link but omits the brand name in the generated text. The study reveals that 62% of brand citations are functionally anonymous, and that different LLMs exhibit opposite linking/mentioning behaviors.
- **Key Takeaways:**
    - **Ghost Citation Metrics:** 61.7% of all domain appearances are ghost citations (cited with a link, but not named). Only 13.2% achieve both a link and a brand mention.
    - **Chatbot Linking Divergence:** Gemini is conversational (names brands in 83.7% of outputs, links in only 21.4%), while ChatGPT is academic (links in 87% of outputs, names brands in only 20.7%).
    - **Aggregators vs. Strong Brands:** Information directories and aggregators (Medium, Wikipedia, Harvard) are cited frequently but remain unnamed. Strong consumer brands get mentioned in text even without links.
    - **Content Strategy Reframe:** Comparative, evaluative, and recommendation content (reviews, product matrices) drives brand mentions, whereas purely informational content ("how-to", "what-is") is parsed and served anonymously.
